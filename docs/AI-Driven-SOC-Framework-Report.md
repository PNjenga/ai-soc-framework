INTRODUCTION
1.1 Background of the Study
Security Operations Centers (SOCs) are central to organizational cybersecurity because they monitor, analyze, and respond to threats across enterprise environments. However, modern SOCs operate in increasingly complex conditions shaped by hybrid cloud services, remote workforces, identity platforms, endpoint systems, and Internet of Things devices. These environments generate large volumes of security telemetry that can improve visibility, but they also increase the burden on analysts. Traditional Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) platforms that depend heavily on fixed rules and signatures often struggle to detect fileless malware, polymorphic threats, living-off-the-land techniques, and multi-stage Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs) (Alshamrani et al., 2019; Khraisat et al., 2019). As a result, SOC teams may experience alert fatigue, delayed response, and limited time for proactive threat hunting.
1.2 Problem Statement
The problem addressed in this project is the misalignment between current AI capabilities and SOC operational needs. Although AI can support faster detection and prioritization, many AI-enabled security systems remain difficult for analysts to interpret and govern. This misalignment appears in three primary areas: excessive alert volume and high false-positive rates, limited interpretability of automated decisions, and trust or governance gaps caused by opaque black-box systems. Stakeholders affected by these issues include SOC analysts, incident responders, Chief Information Security Officers (CISOs), compliance teams, and executive leadership. Without a framework that combines intelligent detection, explainable outputs, and human-in-the-loop governance, organizations may struggle to use AI effectively while preserving accountability and trust.

1.3 Objectives of the Study
The main objective of this study is to design an AI-driven SOC framework that improves threat detection, explainability, and governance. The study specifically aims to examine how machine learning can support SOC alert prioritization, explain how XAI methods can increase analyst trust in automated decisions, and propose governance controls that preserve human oversight during AI-assisted incident response. Additional objectives include mapping the framework to MITRE ATT&CK-informed context, defining the major system components, identifying expected outputs and evaluation metrics, and outlining a practical foundation for future prototype implementation.
1.4 Scope and Limitations
The scope of this study is limited to the conceptual design of an AI-driven SOC framework. The project focuses on system architecture, algorithmic design, data flow, explainability, governance, expected outputs, design diagrams, and evaluation metrics. It does not implement a production-ready SOC platform or test the framework in a live enterprise environment. The study assumes the use of public or simulated cybersecurity datasets rather than proprietary organizational data. Because the framework is conceptual, final performance values for detection precision, recall, false-positive reduction, and triage time are proposed as evaluation targets for future prototype testing rather than reported as completed experimental results. Additional limitations include possible model drift, adversarial manipulation, dataset bias, and the need for analyst feedback to validate explanation usefulness.

LITERATURE REVIEW
2.1 Overview of Related Work
Existing research shows that Security Operations Centers face increasing pressure from sophisticated attacks, high alert volumes, heterogeneous data sources, and analyst workload constraints. Traditional intrusion detection and SIEM-based approaches remain valuable for centralized monitoring and log correlation, but they often depend on static rules, known signatures, and manually tuned thresholds. These approaches can be less effective against advanced persistent threats, fileless malware, polymorphic attacks, and living-off-the-land techniques that do not always match known indicators (Alshamrani et al., 2019; Khraisat et al., 2019). Recent research on AI-augmented SOCs also highlights the growing role of AI agents and large language model support in security automation, while reinforcing the need for governance and analyst oversight (Srinivas et al., 2025).
Machine learning has been widely studied as a way to improve intrusion detection and security monitoring. Data mining and machine-learning methods can identify hidden patterns in large telemetry datasets and support anomaly detection, classification, and prioritization of suspicious activity (Buczak & Guven, 2016; Khraisat et al., 2019). In the context of this project, XGBoost is relevant for structured event classification because it performs well with tabular features, while LSTM models are relevant for sequence-based behavior analysis because they can model temporal patterns in user, endpoint, and network activity (Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997).
Explainable Artificial Intelligence is also a major theme in the literature because security analysts must understand the reasoning behind automated alerts before taking action. SHAP and LIME are widely used model-interpretability methods that identify influential features behind a prediction and support analyst trust (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2016). Explainability is especially important in high-stakes cybersecurity decisions, where opaque outputs can increase uncertainty, reduce adoption, and complicate accountability (Abdul Mutalib et al., 2024).
Research on adversarial machine learning further emphasizes that AI-based security systems must be designed for robustness. Attackers may attempt evasion, data poisoning, model extraction, or other adversarial methods that weaken detection reliability (Biggio & Roli, 2018; Goodfellow et al., 2018; Vassilev et al., 2025). Related work also supports the use of structured cyber knowledge bases such as MITRE ATT&CK to provide a common language for describing adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures. Semantic mapping tools that connect vulnerabilities to ATT&CK techniques can strengthen detection logic, improve analyst context, and support standardized incident reporting (Basel et al., 2023).
2.2 Comparative Analysis of Existing Systems
Existing SOC technologies can be compared across detection capability, explainability, analyst workflow support, and governance maturity. Rule-based SIEM platforms are useful for compliance reporting, log aggregation, and known-pattern detection, but they often produce high alert volumes and require substantial manual tuning. Traditional intrusion detection systems can identify known network or host-based threats, but they may struggle with novel attacks and evolving adversary behavior. Machine-learning-based detection systems improve adaptability, but many implementations emphasize predictive performance without providing clear explanations or governance controls.

However, these capabilities are often implemented separately. This project therefore combines them into a unified framework where detection, explanation, governance, and response are designed as connected components rather than isolated tools.
2.3 Research Gap Identification
The literature reveals a clear gap between AI research and practical SOC implementation. Many studies focus on improving detection accuracy, but fewer address how AI outputs should be explained, governed, audited, and integrated into analyst workflows. In operational cybersecurity environments, an accurate alert is not sufficient if analysts cannot understand why it was generated, determine whether it should be trusted, or justify the response action to leadership, auditors, or compliance teams.
A second gap is the limited integration of explainability and governance. XAI methods such as SHAP and LIME explain model behavior, but they do not automatically create accountable SOC workflows. Governance requires approval gates, audit logs, override tracking, response authorization, and feedback loops. Similarly, SOAR platforms can automate actions, but automation without human validation may increase operational and ethical risk. The proposed framework addresses this gap by placing explainable AI inside a human-in-the-loop governance model.
A third gap concerns traceability and continuous improvement. Existing systems may record alerts and incidents, but they do not always preserve a complete chain from raw telemetry to model prediction, explanation, analyst decision, response action, and audit evidence. Without this traceability, organizations may struggle to evaluate model performance, identify false positives, detect model drift, or demonstrate responsible AI use. This project addresses the gap by proposing a four-layer architecture supported by design diagrams, module traceability, evaluation metrics, and appendix materials that connect detection, explanation, response, and auditability.
Therefore, the research gap can be summarized as the need for an integrated AI-driven SOC framework that improves detection while also providing explainability, analyst oversight, ATT&CK-informed context, governed response, and auditable decision records. This project contributes to that gap by proposing a professional and academic design that treats AI as an analyst-support mechanism rather than a replacement for human judgment.

3.1 System Architecture
The proposed system design is a four-layer AI-driven Security Operations Center (SOC) framework that integrates detection, explanation, and governance into a single operational workflow. The architecture is designed to address three major SOC challenges identified in the project: excessive alert volume, limited interpretability of automated decisions, and weak governance around AI-assisted incident response. Instead of treating machine learning as a standalone detection tool, the framework places AI inside a controlled security operations process where analysts remain responsible for final decisions.

The second layer, AI-Based Threat Detection, applies behavioral analytics, XGBoost classification, LSTM sequence modeling, anomaly scoring, and MITRE ATT&CK mapping (Basel et al., 2023; Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). The third layer, Explainable AI Triage, uses SHAP and LIME to generate feature-importance explanations, confidence scores, and analyst-readable summaries (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2016). The fourth layer, Governance and Incident Response, requires analyst validation for high-impact actions, records audit logs, and supports controlled integration with SOAR playbooks, consistent with responsible AI risk-management principles (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023).
3.2 Methodologies and Tools Used
The methodology uses a conceptual systems design approach. First, scholarly and industry literature is reviewed to identify SOC challenges, intrusion detection methods, explainable AI practices, adversarial machine learning risks, and AI governance principles. Second, stakeholder needs and functional requirements are defined for SOC analysts, incident responders, CISOs, compliance teams, and executive leadership. Third, the project translates those requirements into a modular architecture that connects data collection, AI detection, explainability, analyst validation, and auditable response.
3.2.1 Algorithmic Design
The algorithmic design uses complementary machine-learning methods rather than a single black-box detector. XGBoost is selected for structured security features such as event frequency, source address patterns, authentication behavior, device metadata, and alert attributes because gradient tree boosting is effective for structured classification tasks (Chen & Guestrin, 2016). LSTM models are selected for sequence-based behavior analysis because they can evaluate event order and temporal patterns across user, endpoint, or network activity (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997). These models are supported by anomaly scoring, threshold tuning, confidence scoring, and MITRE ATT&CK technique mapping (Basel et al., 2023). SHAP and LIME are used as explainability tools to show why the model assigned risk to an event or alert (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2016). Governance tools include human-in-the-loop approval, audit logging, incident review, and controlled SOAR playbook execution.
3.2.1.1 Detection and Triage Logic
The project logic follows a security operations workflow: collect telemetry, normalize events, extract relevant features, score events through AI models, explain high-risk outputs, map findings to ATT&CK tactics and techniques, route alerts to analysts, require validation for high-impact decisions, execute response actions only under governance controls, and store decision evidence for audit and future model improvement. This logic ensures that detection, explanation, review, response, and auditability remain connected across the system.
3.2.2 Data Collection Methods
The data collection approach is designed for a conceptual prototype and avoids the use of proprietary or personally identifiable enterprise data. The framework assumes the use of public or simulated cybersecurity datasets that represent endpoint logs, authentication records, network-flow data, firewall events, cloud audit logs, identity events, and threat intelligence indicators. These sources are appropriate because they reflect common SOC telemetry categories while reducing privacy, confidentiality, and compliance risks.
Collected data would be processed through a secure ETL pipeline that validates schemas, removes duplicate records, aligns timestamps, normalizes event formats, and enriches records with contextual information such as asset type, user identity category, source reputation, and MITRE ATT&CK technique mapping. Privacy-preserving controls such as data minimization, tokenization, and encryption are included to ensure that the prototype design supports responsible handling of security telemetry.
3.2.3 Analysis Techniques
The analysis techniques include classification, anomaly detection, sequence analysis, explainability review, threat-context mapping, governance assessment, and performance evaluation. Classification supports the identification of known or likely threat categories, while anomaly detection highlights behavior that differs from expected baselines. Sequence analysis helps identify suspicious patterns over time, such as repeated failed logins followed by privilege escalation or unusual outbound connections.
Explainability analysis is conducted using SHAP and LIME to determine which features influenced the model decision. The results are translated into analyst-readable summaries so that SOC users can understand the reason for each alert. Governance analysis examines whether high-impact response actions include analyst approval, override justification, audit logging, and post-incident review. Evaluation techniques include measuring detection precision, recall, false-positive rate, mean time to triage, explanation usefulness, governance compliance, model drift, and audit completeness.
The architecture support materials in Appendix A: Architecture and System Design Support provide additional detail on how these tools map to the framework’s layers, modules, and traceability flow.
3.3 Data Flow and System Components
The data flow begins when raw telemetry enters the ingestion layer from endpoint detection tools, cloud logs, identity systems, firewalls, network sensors, and threat intelligence sources. The ingestion module performs schema validation, deduplication, timestamp alignment, parsing, enrichment, and normalization. Normalized events are then passed into the feature engineering module, where security-relevant attributes are extracted, including login frequency, geographic anomalies, process behavior, network destination patterns, privilege use, and known indicators of compromise.
After feature extraction, the detection module evaluates the event stream using classification, anomaly detection, and sequence analysis. Structured event features are processed by XGBoost, while time-based patterns are processed by LSTM models. The detection layer produces risk scores, confidence levels, suspected attack categories, and ATT&CK technique mappings. These outputs move to the explainability module, where SHAP and LIME identify the most influential features behind each prediction. The triage module then converts the technical model output into analyst-readable summaries that describe what was detected, why it was flagged, how severe it appears, and what evidence supports the alert.
The governance and response module receives the explained alert and determines the next workflow step. Low-risk alerts may be queued for monitoring, medium-risk alerts may be assigned to an analyst for triage, and high-risk alerts require explicit human validation before containment or remediation. If approved, controlled SOAR playbooks may isolate endpoints, disable accounts, block indicators, or escalate incidents. All decisions, explanations, overrides, and response actions are stored in an audit log. This feedback is used for post-incident review, model monitoring, retraining decisions, and governance improvement.
The main system components are the telemetry connector, normalization engine, feature engineering pipeline, AI detection engine, ATT&CK mapping component, explainability service, analyst triage interface, governance control layer, SOAR integration point, and audit repository. Each component supports a specific part of the SOC workflow while maintaining traceability from the original event to the final analyst decision.

RESULTS
4.1 Implementation Overview
The implementation overview describes how the proposed AI-driven Security Operations Center (SOC) framework would be developed and tested as a prototype. Although this project is conceptual, the implementation plan is structured as a practical sequence of modules that can be built, integrated, and evaluated. The prototype would begin with a telemetry ingestion layer that accepts representative security events from public or simulated datasets, including endpoint logs, authentication records, firewall events, cloud audit logs, and network-flow data. These inputs would be normalized into a consistent event format so that downstream detection and explanation components can process them reliably.
The second implementation stage would prepare the data for analysis through parsing, deduplication, timestamp alignment, feature extraction, and enrichment with threat intelligence or MITRE ATT&CK context. Relevant features may include login frequency, failed authentication patterns, unusual geographic access, endpoint process behavior, command-line activity, network destination reputation, privilege escalation indicators, and abnormal data movement. These features would be passed to the AI detection layer, where structured events are analyzed with XGBoost and sequential behavior is analyzed with LSTM models (Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997).
The third implementation stage would generate model outputs such as risk scores, confidence values, anomaly labels, suspected attack categories, and ATT&CK technique mappings. These outputs would not be sent directly to analysts as raw predictions. Instead, they would move into the explainable AI triage layer, where SHAP and LIME would identify the most influential features behind each prediction (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2016). The system would then produce analyst-readable explanations that describe why the alert was generated, what evidence supports the alert, and what response should be considered.
The final implementation stage would connect the explained alerts to a governed response workflow. Low-risk alerts may be queued for monitoring, medium-risk alerts may be assigned to an analyst for investigation, and high-risk alerts would require explicit human approval before containment or remediation. If approved, a controlled SOAR playbook could isolate an endpoint, disable a suspicious account, block an indicator, or escalate the case. Every prediction, explanation, analyst decision, override, and response action would be stored in an audit repository. This implementation approach preserves the original project work by showing how the framework can move from conceptual design to an operational prototype.
The key implementation deliverables are the four-layer architecture, normalized telemetry pipeline, AI detection logic, explainability layer, governance workflow, risk register, resource justification table, and evaluation metrics. Together, these deliverables show how the design can guide future prototype development and testing.

DISCUSSION
5.1 Interpretation of Results
The results indicate that the proposed AI-driven SOC framework is most valuable when interpreted as a decision-support system rather than a fully autonomous security platform. The design outputs show that machine learning can improve alert prioritization and detection coverage, but these benefits become operationally meaningful only when paired with explainability, analyst review, and governance controls. This aligns with responsible AI risk-management principles that emphasize transparency, accountability, human oversight, and continuous monitoring (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023). Therefore, the main result is not only the use of AI for threat detection, but the creation of an accountable workflow that connects model predictions to analyst judgment and organizational oversight.
The expected outputs, screenshots, and evaluation metrics further support this interpretation. The alert triage record, explainability panel, MITRE ATT&CK mapping, governance review screen, and audit log show how the framework can reduce analyst uncertainty by presenting risk scores together with evidence and justification. The design also shows that high-impact response actions should not be triggered solely by model output. Instead, analyst approval, override documentation, and audit records ensure that the system remains transparent and reviewable.
5.2 Comparison with Literature Findings
The findings align with the literature’s view that AI can improve SOC operations only when detection capability is paired with transparency and governance. Prior research shows that traditional SIEM and intrusion detection systems struggle with alert volume, false positives, and sophisticated attack techniques (Alshamrani et al., 2019; Khraisat et al., 2019). The proposed framework responds to these concerns by using AI-assisted detection to support alert prioritization and behavioral analysis. This is consistent with machine-learning intrusion detection research, which identifies data-driven models as useful for recognizing complex patterns in large security datasets (Buczak & Guven, 2016).
The results also align with explainable AI literature. SHAP and LIME are designed to make model predictions more interpretable by identifying influential features behind each prediction (Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Ribeiro et al., 2016). This project applies those concepts directly to SOC triage by proposing explanation panels, analyst-readable summaries, and evidence-based alert records. The framework also reflects responsible AI guidance by including human-in-the-loop approval, audit logging, and governance review, which support the risk-management principles emphasized by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (2023). Compared with existing systems discussed in the literature review, the proposed framework contributes by integrating detection, explainability, ATT&CK context, governed response, and traceability into one unified design.
5.3 Implications and Limitations
The project has several practical implications for SOC modernization. First, it shows that AI should be implemented as part of a broader operational workflow rather than as an isolated prediction engine. Second, it emphasizes that explanations must be designed for analyst use, not only for technical model interpretation. Third, it demonstrates that governance features such as approval gates, audit logs, override tracking, and response authorization are essential when AI recommendations may influence security actions. These implications are important for SOC analysts, incident responders, CISOs, compliance teams, and executive decision-makers because they connect technical detection improvements with accountability and organizational trust.
Several limitations remain. The framework is conceptual and has not yet been implemented as a production-ready system, so detection precision, recall, false-positive reduction, and mean time to triage cannot be claimed as measured results. Public or simulated datasets may not fully represent enterprise SOC environments, and model performance may degrade over time because of concept drift. The framework may also face adversarial risks such as evasion, poisoning, and telemetry manipulation (Biggio & Roli, 2018; Goodfellow et al., 2018; Vassilev et al., 2025). In addition, explanation usefulness must be validated with SOC analysts because technically accurate explanations may still be difficult to interpret during high-pressure operations.
5.4 Future Work
Future work should focus on moving the framework from conceptual design to prototype implementation. A prototype should ingest public or simulated cybersecurity datasets, normalize telemetry, extract features, generate risk scores, apply SHAP and LIME explanations, map alerts to MITRE ATT&CK techniques, and route alerts through a human-in-the-loop review process. The prototype should then be evaluated using the metrics identified in Section 4.3, including detection precision, recall, false-positive rate, mean time to triage, explanation usefulness, governance compliance, model drift monitoring, and audit completeness.
Additional future research should include analyst usability testing, adversarial robustness evaluation, privacy and fairness assessment, and comparison between traditional SIEM triage and AI-assisted triage. Future versions of the framework could also include more detailed SOAR playbooks, expanded ATT&CK coverage analysis, model monitoring dashboards, and governance policies for periodic review. These next steps would help determine whether the proposed framework can improve real-world SOC efficiency while maintaining explainability, trust, and responsible oversight.


 
CONCLUSION
6.1 Summary of Main Findings
This project developed a conceptual AI-driven Security Operations Center (SOC) framework designed to improve threat detection, explainability, and governance. The main finding is that AI can provide meaningful value to SOC operations when it is integrated into a structured workflow that includes data ingestion, machine-learning-based detection, explainable triage, human validation, governed response, and audit logging. The proposed four-layer architecture demonstrates how raw telemetry can be transformed into prioritized, interpretable, and accountable security alerts rather than opaque model outputs.
6.1.1 Implications of the Findings
The findings have practical and strategic implications. Practically, the framework supports analysts through risk scoring, explainable alert summaries, ATT&CK mapping, and dashboard-based triage. Strategically, it shows that organizations can use AI to improve SOC efficiency without removing human judgment from high-impact security decisions. Approval gates, audit logs, override tracking, and continuous feedback help ensure that AI-assisted security operations remain transparent and accountable.
6.2 Contributions to the Field
The primary contribution of this project is a professional and academic blueprint for SOC modernization that combines AI-assisted detection, explainable AI, human-in-the-loop governance, SOAR-supported response, and auditability in one integrated design. Unlike approaches that focus only on detection accuracy, this framework emphasizes operational trust, traceability, and responsible AI use. It contributes to the cybersecurity field by showing how AI can augment SOC analysts, reduce alert fatigue, improve decision quality, and support accountable incident response. Future implementation and evaluation can extend this work from conceptual design to a tested prototype that measures detection performance, analyst workload reduction, explanation usefulness, and governance effectiveness.
